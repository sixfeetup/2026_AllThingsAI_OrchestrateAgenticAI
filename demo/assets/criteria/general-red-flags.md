# General Red Flags

Criteria for identifying common red flags in contract and procurement documents. Each criterion below is evaluated independently against the document text.

## Impossible or Missing Dates

### What to look for
Calendar dates that do not exist (e.g., February 30, June 31), deadlines referencing past dates at time of signing, and timelines described with vague language instead of concrete dates (e.g., "promptly," "with all deliberate speed," "in due course").

### Why it matters
Contracts with impossible dates are unenforceable on their face for those provisions. Vague timelines create ambiguity about when performance is actually due, making it nearly impossible to determine whether a party is in breach. Both situations invite disputes and leave the parties without meaningful schedule commitments.

### Red flag indicators
- Any date that does not exist on the calendar (e.g., February 30, April 31)
- Delivery or milestone dates described only with qualitative language and no specific date
- Phrases like "shall commence promptly," "with all deliberate speed," or "as soon as practicable" used in place of a defined start date
- Absence of a project schedule or timeline exhibit referenced elsewhere in the agreement

## Incorrect Party Names

### What to look for
Legal entity names that are inconsistent across the document, misspelled party names, or references that use a similar but incorrect entity name (e.g., substituting a word in the company name). Compare every mention of each party's legal name against the name defined in the preamble.

### Why it matters
An incorrect legal entity name can create ambiguity about who is actually bound by the contract. In a dispute, a party could argue that obligations assigned to a misspelled or incorrect entity name do not apply to them. It may also indicate sloppy drafting that could signal deeper problems throughout the agreement.

### Red flag indicators
- A party name that differs from the name established in the preamble or recitals
- Subtle word substitutions in entity names (e.g., "Up" vs. "Down," "East" vs. "West")
- Inconsistent use of legal suffixes (Inc., LLC, Ltd.) across the document
- Defined terms for parties that are used inconsistently

## One-Sided Indemnification

### What to look for
Indemnification clauses where one party bears all or nearly all of the risk while the other party's indemnification obligations are minimal, capped, or absent. Look for asymmetry in the scope of indemnified claims, differences in carve-outs or limitations, and whether mutual indemnification is truly mutual in practice.

### Why it matters
Dramatically asymmetric indemnification shifts virtually all litigation and liability risk onto one party. A consultant or vendor who indemnifies against broad categories of claims while the client's indemnification is narrow or nonexistent takes on disproportionate financial exposure. This imbalance can be existential for smaller companies.

### Red flag indicators
- One party's indemnification section is substantially longer or broader than the other's
- Indemnification obligations that cover "any and all claims" for one party but only specific, narrow claims for the other
- Absence of mutual indemnification when both parties have meaningful obligations
- One party indemnifies for third-party IP claims, data breaches, and general negligence while the other indemnifies only for breach of payment terms
- No cap on indemnification liability for one party while the other has explicit caps

## Buried Material Terms

### What to look for
Substantive provisions that appear in sections where they would not normally be expected. Key terms related to renewal, termination, exclusivity, or financial obligations that are placed in general or miscellaneous sections rather than in the dedicated section addressing that topic.

### Why it matters
Placing material terms in unexpected locations — such as burying an auto-renewal clause in General Provisions instead of the Term and Termination section — can cause a party to overlook critical obligations. Whether intentional or not, this drafting pattern obscures terms that significantly affect the parties' rights and obligations.

### Red flag indicators
- Auto-renewal or evergreen clauses appearing outside of the Term and Termination section
- Payment or fee escalation terms buried in definitions or general provisions
- Non-compete or exclusivity obligations placed in miscellaneous sections
- Termination conditions or penalties located outside the termination section
- Any provision in a "General" or "Miscellaneous" section that creates a substantive obligation rather than a procedural or interpretive rule

## Undefined Obligations

### What to look for
Performance obligations described using qualitative language without measurable criteria, acceptance standards, or objective benchmarks. Watch for phrases like "reasonable effort," "best effort," "industry standard," or "satisfactory" that are used repeatedly without definition or reference to an external standard.

### Why it matters
When obligations lack measurable criteria, neither party can objectively determine whether performance has been achieved. This creates a breeding ground for disputes: the performing party believes it has met its obligations while the receiving party disagrees. Without defined standards, enforcement becomes subjective and unpredictable.

### Red flag indicators
- Repeated use of "reasonable effort" or "best effort" without defining what constitutes reasonable or best
- Acceptance criteria described as "to Client's satisfaction" without objective standards
- Deliverables described in general terms with no reference to specifications, SOWs, or acceptance test procedures
- Service levels promised without quantitative metrics or measurement methodology
- The same vague qualifier (e.g., "reasonable") used three or more times to describe different obligations

## Hidden Unusual Provisions

### What to look for
Non-standard, bizarre, or unexpected obligations buried within otherwise routine boilerplate sections. These are clauses that impose unusual requirements — obligations that have nothing to do with the services being contracted — tucked into sections a reviewer might skim or skip entirely, such as Force Majeure, Notices, or General Provisions.

### Why it matters
Unusual provisions hidden in boilerplate sections can create real, enforceable obligations that a party never intended to accept. Because reviewers often treat boilerplate as standard language, these clauses can go undetected until a dispute arises. They may also signal bad faith in drafting.

### Red flag indicators
- Obligations unrelated to the contract's subject matter appearing in boilerplate sections
- Specific deliverables or performance requirements embedded in Force Majeure, Severability, or similar sections
- Clauses requiring delivery of goods or services that are not part of the contracted scope
- Any provision that would cause a reasonable reviewer to do a double-take
- Quantified obligations (specific quantities, dollar amounts, frequencies) appearing in sections that typically contain only general legal principles
