# IP and Ownership Provisions

## Exclusive vs Non-Exclusive IP Assignment

### What to look for

Compare every clause that addresses ownership of work product, deliverables, or intellectual property created during the engagement. Identify whether the contract assigns IP **exclusively** to one party in one place and then grants only a **non-exclusive license** (or retains ownership for the other party) elsewhere. Pay close attention to modifiers like "irrevocably," "exclusively," "solely," and "all right, title, and interest" versus "non-exclusive," "revocable," or "limited license."

### Why it matters

A contract that simultaneously assigns exclusive ownership to the Client and lets the Consultant retain IP creates a direct legal contradiction. Neither party can rely on the agreement to know who actually owns the work. In a dispute, a court would have to resolve the ambiguity -- often in a way neither party intended. This is not a gray area; mutually exclusive ownership terms make the contract internally inconsistent.

### Red flag indicators

- One section assigns "all right, title, and interest" to the Client while another says the Consultant "retains" IP rights.
- The word "exclusive" appears in an assignment clause but a separate clause grants only a "non-exclusive" license back to the Client.
- An assignment is described as "irrevocable" in one section while another section describes a license as "revocable."
- Two or more sections each claim a different party owns the same category of work product.

## Pre-Existing IP Protection

### What to look for

Determine whether the contract distinguishes between **newly created work product** and the Consultant's **pre-existing intellectual property** (tools, frameworks, libraries, reusable components, proprietary methodologies). Check whether pre-existing IP is explicitly carved out from any broad assignment clause. Look for a definition of "Pre-Existing IP" or "Consultant Tools" and verify that the assignment-of-work-product language does not inadvertently sweep in materials the Consultant owned before the engagement began.

### Why it matters

Consultants routinely use their own reusable tools and frameworks across multiple client engagements. If a broad assignment clause transfers "all work product" without carving out pre-existing IP, the Consultant could lose rights to tools they built independently -- tools other clients may already depend on. The Consultant's ongoing business viability can hinge on this distinction being explicit and enforceable.

### Red flag indicators

- A blanket assignment of "all work product" or "all intellectual property created in connection with the Services" with no carve-out for pre-existing materials.
- Pre-existing IP is mentioned but the license terms for it are vague, undefined, or conflict with the assignment clause.
- No schedule, exhibit, or definition listing or describing the Consultant's pre-existing IP.
- The contract uses broad language like "all materials developed, conceived, or reduced to practice" without limiting it to materials created *during* and *for* the engagement.

## License Scope Clarity

### What to look for

For every license grant in the contract -- whether for pre-existing IP, work product, or third-party components -- verify that the following terms are **explicitly stated**: scope of permitted use, duration (perpetual, term-limited, or tied to the agreement), exclusivity (exclusive or non-exclusive), sublicensability, and transferability. Flag any license whose terms are described as "as mutually understood," "to be agreed," or similarly deferred to informal or unwritten understandings.

### Why it matters

A license grant that relies on "mutual understanding" rather than explicit written terms is effectively no license at all. Either party can later claim a different understanding of scope, duration, or exclusivity. Courts generally cannot enforce terms the parties never reduced to writing. Vague license language creates the same practical risk as having no license: uncertainty about what the licensee can actually do with the IP.

### Red flag indicators

- Phrases like "as mutually understood by the Parties," "as agreed," or "to be determined" in place of concrete license terms.
- A license grant that specifies scope or exclusivity in one dimension but leaves others undefined (e.g., states "non-exclusive" but says nothing about duration or transferability).
- License terms that differ across sections of the contract for the same category of IP without explanation.
- No explicit statement of whether the license survives termination of the agreement.
