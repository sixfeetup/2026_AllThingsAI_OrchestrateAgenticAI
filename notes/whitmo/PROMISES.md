# Promises Made in the Pitch

## Explicit Promises

These are directly stated commitments to the audience.

### 1. Teach context engineering tactics
> "How to chunk documents and tasks, frame targeted prompts, and apply
> checklists so each sub-task has clear acceptance criteria and traceability."

Commits to covering: document chunking, prompt framing, checklist design,
acceptance criteria, and traceability.

### 2. Teach agentic workflow strategies
> "How to turn a vague 'review this' into a structured series of sub-jobs,
> including clause-level analysis, routing, and validation so nothing is
> silently skipped."

Commits to showing how to decompose review tasks into sub-jobs with routing and
validation.

### 3. Deliver a live "No-Miss Review" demo
> "A live, clause-level walkthrough of an AI-driven contract review that shows
> task graphs, specialized agents, routing, and validation, plus direct
> parallels for engineering workflows."

Commits to a working live demo that includes task graphs, specialized agents,
routing, validation, and explicit mapping to engineering workflows.

### 4. Provide an actionable playbook
> "Walk away with an actionable playbook to make AI reliable, auditable, and
> ready for high-stakes enterprise use."

Commits to attendees leaving with something they can apply immediately, not just
concepts.

### 5. Address reliability, auditability, and high-stakes readiness

The pitch repeatedly frames AI as needing to be "trusted," "dependable,"
"reliable," "auditable," and safe for "high-stakes" use. These words set a bar:
the talk must show how to actually reach that standard.

### 6. Cover both contract/policy review AND software engineering workflows
> "...contracts, policies, audits, and software development workflows such as
> code review and test planning."

The pitch scopes across domains. It promises relevant content for both
business/legal reviewers and engineers.


## Implicit Promises

These are expectations created by framing, tone, credentials, and structure,
even though they are never directly stated.

### 7. The speaker has solved the problem being described
The pitch opens with a shared pain point ("You are not alone") and positions the
speaker as having answers. The audience will expect demonstrated expertise, not
a survey of the landscape.

### 8. Existing tools and approaches are insufficient
> "New tool frameworks and MCP-style integrations are multiplying calls and
> windows, but not reliability."

This implicitly promises the talk will present something better than current
tooling. If the solution relies heavily on MCP or standard chat-style tooling,
it will feel contradictory.

### 9. The demo will be convincing, not a toy example
"No-Miss Review" is a strong claim. The audience will expect the demo to handle
a realistic contract (or comparable artifact), not a cherry-picked trivial case.

### 10. Practical over theoretical
The pitch uses "practical," "actionable," and "playbook." The implicit promise
is that this is not an architecture talk or a research summary. Attendees expect
to be able to replicate the approach.

### 11. The approach generalizes beyond the demo domain
> "...plus direct parallels for engineering workflows."

The implicit promise is that the patterns shown for contract review transfer
cleanly to code review, test planning, and policy review. The talk needs to make
these parallels concrete, not hand-wave.

### 12. The approach addresses governance and audit concerns
> "...audit anxiety, governance gaps..."

By naming these as problems, the pitch promises the solution will address how to
produce audit trails and satisfy governance requirements. Not just "better
prompts" but provable process.

### 13. The "playbook" is more than a slide
"Actionable playbook" implies something attendees take home: a template, a repo,
a checklist document, or at minimum a clear enough process to reconstruct. A
talk that ends with "and that's the approach" without a takeaway artifact will
feel like an undelivered promise.

### 14. The orchestration taxonomy will resolve into a recommendation
The "1st blush" notes list four orchestration styles (conductor, relentless
loop, swarm, spec-driven) with pros/cons. The implicit promise to the audience
is that the talk will guide them toward which approach to use when, not just
enumerate options.

### 15. "Eventual consistency" will be substantiated
The notes frame code generation as easy but validation as hard, and claim
"testing validity and impact > initial correctness." The audience will expect a
concrete demonstration of how iterative validation converges on a correct
result, not just the assertion that it does.

### 16. Safety and containment practices will be addressed concretely
> "practices around containment, isolation, modularity, and transparency become
> much more important"

Raising speed-of-failure and blast radius as concerns implicitly promises the
talk will show specific containment techniques, not just acknowledge the risk.
